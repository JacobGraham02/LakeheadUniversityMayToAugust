	A reflexive pronoun is a type of pronoun that reflects the action of the verb back onto the subject. 
In French, reflexive pronouns are used with reflexive verbs to indicate that the subject is performing the action on itself.
	A pronominal verb is a verb that is accompanied by a reflexive pronoun. Pronominal verbs fall into three major classes based on their meaning:
reflexive, idiomatic, and reciprocol. To conjugate pronominal verbs in the present tense, we must pay attention to both the pronoun and the verb form.


	Pronominal verbs often express reflexive actions, that is, the subject performs the action on itself. If the subject performs the action on someone
else, the verb is not reflexive. Below is a list of common reflexive verbs:
	1. s'asseoir; 		to sit (down)
	2. s'appeler; 		to be called 
	3. s'arrêter; 		to stop 
	4. se brosser; 		to brush

	10. se réveiller; 	to wake up
	
	In the context of language or grammar, the term "reflexive meaning" refers to the idea that a verb is used to indicate that the subject is performing the 
action on itself. This indicates a reflexive relationship between the subject and the action. In French, reflexive verbs are used in such cases and require 
the use of reflexive pronouns. The reflexive pronouns indicate that the subject is both the doer and the receiver of the action. The reflexive meaning adds 
a level of self-reference or self-involvement to the action expressed by the verb.

	French must explicitly state reflexive meaning by using a reflexive pronoun. 
	E.g. Est-ce que Tex se rase? Mais non, Tex, c'est un tatou. Il n'a pas de cheveux. - Does Tex shave? Why no, Tex is an armadillo. He has no hair.

Partitive articles are a type of articles used in French to indicate an unspecified or unknown quantity of something. They are used when referring to a portion, part, or some amount of a mass noun or an uncountable noun. 
	The partitive articles in French are as follows:
		"Du" is used before a masculine singular noun, for example:
			Je mange du pain. (I eat some bread.)
			Il boit du café. (He drinks some coffee.)
		"De la" is used before a feminine singular noun, for example:
			Elle boit de la limonade. (She drinks some lemonade.)
			J'ai acheté de la farine. (I bought some flour.)
		"De l'" is used before a singular noun that starts with a vowel sound, for example:
			Elle a bu de l'eau. (She drank some water.)
			Il a mangé de l'orange. (He ate some orange.)
		"Des" is used before plural nouns, regardless of gender, for example:
			Nous avons des fruits. (We have some fruits.)
			Ils ont des livres. (They have some books.)
	Partitive articles are used to indicate an indefinite or non-specific quantity. They are often used with verbs like "manger" (to eat), "boire" (to drink), "acheter" (to buy), and others when referring to an unspecified 
	amount of something.

The contraction "au" is used in French when combining the preposition "à" (to, at) with the masculine singular 
definite article "le" (the).
Au, à, la, à l', and aux are all ways of saying 'to the' or 'at the' 
Au is commonly used when referring to going to or being at a specific masculine singular place:
	Going to a masculine singular country: Je vais au Canada. (I am going to Canada.)
	Going to a masculine singular city: Je vais au restaurant. (I am going to the restaurant.)
	Going to a masculine singular institution: Je vais au musée. (I am going to the museum.)
	Being at a masculine singular place: Je suis au bureau. (I am at the office.)
In these cases, "au" replaces "à" + "le" to indicate the specific location.

	"Au" is used before masculine singular nouns: au parc (to the park), au restaurant (at the restaurant).
	"À" is used before feminine singular nouns: à la plage (to the beach), à l'école (at the school).
	"À l'" is used before singular nouns starting with a vowel sound: à l'hôpital (to the hospital), 
	 à l'université (at the university).
	"Aux" is used before plural nouns: aux magasins (to the stores), aux fêtes (at the parties).

	Here are some common situations where "au" is used:
		Expressing location or destination:
			Je vais au cinéma. (I am going to the cinema.)
			Il est au travail. (He is at work.)
			Nous allons au restaurant. (We are going to the restaurant.)
		Referring to a specific time:
			Je te verrai au dîner. (I will see you at dinner.)
			Rendez-vous au matin. (Meet you in the morning.)
			Je pars au printemps. (I am leaving in the spring.)
		Indicating possession or association:
			C'est la clé au trousseau. (It's the key to the keychain.)
			Il a pris la voiture au père. (He took the car from his father.)
			Les droits d'auteur au livre. (The copyrights to the book.)
	Note that "au" is specifically used with masculine singular nouns. If the noun is feminine singular, "à la" is used instead. For example, "à la maison" (at the house).


The ending "aux" is added in French when combining the preposition "à" (to, at) with the plural definite article "les" (the).
	Here are some common situations where "aux" is used:
		Expressing location or destination:
			Nous allons aux magasins. (We are going to the shops.)
			Ils habitent aux États-Unis. (They live in the United States.)
			Elle est aux toilettes. (She is in the restroom.)
		Referring to a specific time:
			Les cours commencent aux matins. (Classes start in the mornings.)
			Je te verrai aux vacances d'été. (I will see you during the summer holidays.)
			Il est rentré aux dernières heures de la nuit. (He came back in the early hours of the night.)
		Indicating possession or association:
			Les clés appartiennent aux voisins. (The keys belong to the neighbors.)
			Ils ont donné les cadeaux aux enfants. (They gave the gifts to the children.)
			Les droits d'auteur appartiennent aux auteurs. (The copyrights belong to the authors.)
	Note that "aux" is specifically used with plural nouns. If the noun is masculine singular, "au" is used, and if the noun is feminine singular, "à la" is used.

Why knowing the gender of a French word is important - https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Lrroi3X0Y_g:
	First reason:
		le (the) and un (a) are used in front of a masculine noun. la (the) and une (a) are used in front of a feminine noun. les (the) and des (some) used in front of a plural noun regardless of gender. 
		
		All adjectives agree in number and gender with the noun they qualify. Le stylo vert est petit (the green pen is small).
		If doing the above sentence in feminine form, you would write La fleur verte est petite (the green flower is small). 
		In plural form:
			Les stylos verts sont petits (The green pens are small)
			Les fleurs vertes sont petites (The green flowers are small)
			
		First reason because the gender of a French word determines the order of words in the sentence
		
	Second reason:
		All possessive pronouns and adjectives agree in number and gender with the noun used. 
		Mon stylo est vert (My pen is green). We use 'Mon' because stylo is masculine. The word is 'vert' instead of 'verte' because the word is masculine instead of feminine. 
		By extension, we say 'Le mien est vert' (Mine is green) because stylo is masculine.
		Ma fleur est verte (My flower is green). 
		La mienne est verte (Mine is green). 
		
	Third reason:
		All demonstrative pronouns and adjectives agree in number and gender with the noun used.

Partitive articles are a type of articles used in French to indicate an unspecified or unknown quantity of something. They are used when referring to a portion, part, or some amount of a mass noun or an uncountable noun. 
	The partitive articles in French are as follows:
		"Du" is used before a masculine singular noun, for example:
			Je mange du pain. (I eat some bread.)
			Il boit du café. (He drinks some coffee.)
		"De la" is used before a feminine singular noun, for example:
			Elle boit de la limonade. (She drinks some lemonade.)
			J'ai acheté de la farine. (I bought some flour.)
		"De l'" is used before a singular noun that starts with a vowel sound, for example:
			Elle a bu de l'eau. (She drank some water.)
			Il a mangé de l'orange. (He ate some orange.)
		"Des" is used before plural nouns, regardless of gender, for example:
			Nous avons des fruits. (We have some fruits.)
			Ils ont des livres. (They have some books.)
	Partitive articles are used to indicate an indefinite or non-specific quantity. They are often used with verbs like "manger" (to eat), "boire" (to drink), "acheter" (to buy), and others when referring to an unspecified 
	amount of something.

Au, à, la, à l', and aux are all ways of saying 'to the' or 'at the' 
Au is commonly used when referring to going to or being at a specific masculine singular place:

In these cases, "au" replaces "à" + "le" to indicate the specific location.

	"Au" is used before masculine singular nouns: au parc (to the park), au restaurant (at the restaurant).

	"À l'" is used before singular nouns starting with a vowel sound: à l'hôpital (to the hospital), 
	 à l'université (at the university).
	"Aux" is used before plural nouns: aux magasins (to the stores), aux fêtes (at the parties).

Basic negation is formed by placing ne ... pas around the conjugated verb. Ne becomes n' in front of a verb starting with a vowel or a mute h.
	tu ne vas pas en cours aujourd'hui?
	Tex: Tu ne vas pas en cours aujourd'hui, Tammy? 	  	Tex: You're not going to class today Tammy?
	Tammy: Non. Je ne vais pas bien. Je ne peux pas manger et j'ai mal à la tête. 	  	Tammy: No. I am not well. I cannot eat and I have a headache.
	Tex: Tu n'as pas de chance! Ce soir, il y a une fête chez Edouard! 	  	Tex: That's too bad [literally 'you are not lucky']! Tonight there is a party at Edouard's!
	Tammy: Tu n'es pas d'un grand réconfort, tu sais! 	  	Tammy: You are not a lot of comfort, you know!

In compound tenses, like the passé composé, the ne ... pas are also placed around the conjugated verb, which is the auxiliary, avoir or être. In the periphrastic future, ne ... pas goes around the verb aller.
